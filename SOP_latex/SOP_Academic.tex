\noindent \textbf{1. Introduction}

As a final-year Electrical Engineering student at Hanoi University of Science and Technology (HUST), my research at the Power Grid and Renewable Energy (PGRE) Laboratory focuses on developing distributed mathematical optimization models for decentralized energy management. While this foundation drove my graduation thesis on microgrid resilience, I recognized that classical optimization alone cannot scale to the real-time computational demands of modern power systems. I am pursuing my Ph.D. at UM-Dearborn to engineer scalable, cyber-physical control architectures that overcome these computational bottlenecks, directly advancing my long-term goal of modernizing national grid infrastructures.  

\vspace{1ex}
\noindent \textbf{2. Academic Journey: The Limits of Deterministic Solvers}

Ranking top 3 in HUST's Advanced Program in Electrical Engineering and Renewable Energy, I trained to deconstruct complex grid architectures into tractable engineering problems. Across diverse projects, I approached these challenges from multiple technical dimensions, for instance, by developing predictive models to quantify system uncertainties and prototyping power electronic circuits to evaluate hardware constraints. Maintaining this multi-faceted perspective directs my independent research trajectory and mitigates critical theoretical and physical blind spots. 

My research trajectory reflects a systematic transition from treating power grids as aggregate generation-load balances to modeling them as complex computational graphs.

To address 24-hour load shifting, I formulated a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model integrating Demand Response. While this approach achieved basic economic dispatch, treating the entire grid as a single lumped node reduced physical power flows to purely algebraic accounting. Recognizing that mathematical optimization lacks operational granularity if it omits physical line constraints, I realized the necessity of incorporating multi-bus network topologies. This structural requirement prompted my transition toward decentralized architectures capable of mapping multi-agent network interactions.

To decentralize the system, I decomposed the IEEE 123-bus network into autonomous microgrids and formulated a peer-to-peer (P2P) active power trading framework using the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) and Game Theory. Although this architecture successfully cleared market transactions, neglecting reactive power, voltage limits, and network losses rendered the resulting economic dispatch physically infeasible. I realized that deploying elegant distributed algorithms is operationally invalid if the underlying grid collapses under voltage violations, necessitating a shift toward full AC power flow modeling. This critical operational vulnerability became the direct catalyst for my graduation thesis, where I resolved to bridge algorithmic optimization with rigorous grid physics.

Operationalizing these AC-OPF constraints within multi-agent markets, my manuscript submitted to Sustainable Energy, Grids and Networks (SEGAN) utilized an SOCP-relaxed ADMM framework to clear P2P transactions across radial and meshed distribution networks. Extending this architecture, my graduation thesis employed Model Predictive Control (MPC) to achieve rolling-horizon benefit allocation. While both frameworks guaranteed operational safety, continuously re-optimizing non-linear AC-OPF equations across 15-minute to 1-hour intervals exposed severe computational scaling limits. This intractable processing overhead renders classical solvers unscalable for adaptive intra-day dispatch, establishing the technical necessity for learning-based AI control and directly motivating my decision to pursue a Ph.D.

\vspace{1ex}
\noindent \textbf{3. Current Research Interest: Physics-Informed AI for Smart Grids}

To resolve the dichotomy between physical grid security and learning-based autonomy, my Ph.D. research will focus on architecting Physics-Informed AI frameworks for real-time power system dispatch. While pure data-driven models accelerate computational speeds, their deployment frequently fails because unconstrained black-box policies inherently violate non-linear AC power flow equations and thermal limits. I intend to synthesize these paradigms by deploying my deterministic optimization models in a dual capacity: as exact oracles to generate mathematically rigorous optimal-dispatch datasets, and as differentiable safety filters—leveraging convex relaxations like SOCP—that project AI-generated setpoints into a verifiable feasible region. By embedding exact grid physics into both the offline training and online execution of Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Large Language Model (LLM) agents, I aim to pioneer certifiably safe and physically resilient dispatch architectures.

\vspace{1ex}
\noindent \textbf{4. Program Fit: Synergy with Prof. Van-Hai Bui}

The research trajectory of Prof. Van-Hai Bui directly aligns with my technical background and Ph.D. objectives. Prof. Bui's recent publications on FairMarket-RL and XRL-LLM represent the frontier of integrating LLMs as real-time fairness critics and decision surrogates in multi-agent energy trading. My extensive experience in modeling exact physical constraints---such as voltage bounds in SOCP and sequential dispatch in MPC---serves as the precise prerequisite necessary to build the robust simulation environments and physical safety constraints required for these RL/LLM agents. I possess the coding and simulation stamina necessary to construct multi-microgrid environments, generate mathematically sound training datasets, and independently debug the critical interface where learning algorithms intersect with AC power flow physics. Joining Prof. Bui's group will allow me to integrate my optimization foundation with Safe RL and explainable AI (XAI), ensuring that predictive control agents remain physically resilient.

\vspace{1ex}
\noindent \textbf{5. Career Objective}

Following the completion of my Ph.D., I intend to return to Vietnam to address the fundamental gap between classical power system operations and state-of-the-art AI. By repatriating advanced, AI-driven grid control technologies, my objective is to spearhead the technical modernization of Vietnam's national grid infrastructure, ensuring operational stability and resilience amid the accelerating integration of renewable energy sources. The doctoral program at the University of Michigan-Dearborn provides the rigorous environment necessary to execute this technical vision.
